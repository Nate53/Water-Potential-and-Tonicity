AP/IB Biology — Tonicity, Osmoregulation & Water Potential

Water is life's solvent. Every chemical reaction in your body, every nutrient absorbed by a plant's roots, every signal sent between your neurons — all of it depends on the behavior of water. Understanding how and why water moves is understanding one of biology's most fundamental stories.

Think about your own body: why do doctors insist you stay hydrated? What happens when you drink too much water — a dangerous condition called water intoxication — and your cells begin to swell? Why does an IV bag need to be carefully formulated so it doesn't destroy your red blood cells? The answer is tonicity and water potential.

Now think about plants. During a drought, why do leaves wilt and crops fail? Why can salt water kill a garden? And at the largest scale, why are the shifting rainfall patterns and rising temperatures caused by climate change so threatening to ecosystems worldwide? It all comes back to osmosis — water's movement across membranes — and the maintenance of homeostasis. You might say the answer is, quite literally, at the root of it all.

In this lab, you will explore the physics and biology behind water movement: what drives it, how cells respond, and why it matters — from a single cell to an entire organism.

Water potential (Ψ) is the sum of two components: Ψ = Ψp + Ψs. Solute potential is always negative or zero, and is calculated using Ψs = −iCRT. Pressure potential is typically zero or positive.

Solute Potential: Ψs = −iCRT

i Ionization Constant 1

Number of particles the solute splits into. Sucrose = 1, NaCl = 2, CaCl₂ = 3
